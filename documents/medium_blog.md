# The Actual Masters Experience (USA)

Author: Suvodeep Sinha (AI @ Intel | CS @ UIUC) — Medium, May 22, 2024

I just completed 2 semesters at the University of Illinois Urbana-Champaign as a
Masters student in Computer Science. It was a roller coaster ride with extreme
highs and lows. Through this blog I share insight into the educational degree
itself — the workload, difficulty, and instructor quality.

Note: in UIUC, 400-level courses are open for graduate students and undergrads
(for a lesser credit option) and 500-level courses are graduate-only courses.

## Semester I

### CS 444 Deep Learning for Computer Vision
This was the most challenging course that I had taken this semester. It covered
basic topics from Linear Regression to advanced Transformer and Diffusion
models. Structure: 5 coding assignments, 1 project (equivalent of 3
assignments), 2 exams. Although the assignments were well documented,
implementing them in code was challenging. The exams were math intensive and
quite mentally exhausting but that is expected from a grad Machine Learning
class. The professor and the course material were extremely helpful for
understanding basics and served as a strong foundation for the multiple ML
engineering interviews that I gave. A must-take if you're interested in this
field broadly.

### CS 445 Computational Photography
I took this course to test whether I wanted a deep dive into computer vision and
3D image problems. This class introduces several classical CV problems and does
not involve any form of Machine Learning. The course structure was exactly the
same as CS 444. The coding assignments were very practical/hands-on. Any errors
faced taught the student to solve the problem rather than just ask ChatGPT. I
would rate this a well-balanced course for learning and workload. Beneficial if
you like to research problems specific to CV or aim at an industry role in CV.

### CS 410 Text Information Systems
I had little to no background in text retrieval or NLP and wanted to start
bottom up rather than jumping to LLMs. This course covered text retrieval, text
mining, search engines, recommendation systems and much more. Structure: 15
weekly quizzes, 3 coding assignments, 1 paper review, 1 project, 2 exams.
Although this may seem a lot on paper, it wasn't the case. In-depth
understanding of the concepts was enough for a lot of them. The class was well
paced, beginner friendly, and the content was very easy to follow. This was my
most interactive class and I recommend it to anyone who doesn't know how to
begin with textual data.

### CS 591 CS Colloquium
Not a traditional class but a 1-credit optional course that required students to
attend seminars/talks or conferences throughout the semester and write a brief
report about the major learnings and intriguing questions from those talks.

## Semester II

### CS 568 User-Centered Machine Learning
To better understand design principles, I wanted a course sharing topics in the
HCI realm. We focused on researching AI systems that are easy to use, adapt to
individual needs, and improve user satisfaction. Structure: 2 research paper
readings weekly, 1 final project (45% of the grade). The course was heavily
research-focused. I recommend this course to anyone wanting to experiment with
HCI+NLP fields with a focus on language models.

### CS 510 Advanced Information Retrieval
The advanced version of CS 410 — quite rigorous but very rewarding. It covered
advanced topics in information retrieval: mixture models, statistical models,
and ways to obtain Big Text data. Special focus was given to LLMs and their
applications. Structure: 4 quizzes, 3 assignments, 1 experimental assignment on
LLM and a large codebase, 1 paper/toolkit review, 1 exam, 1 project. Having
taken CS 410 was an added advantage.

### CS 412 Introduction to Data Mining
This course is taken by a lot of undergrads as it covers many topics of
statistics in depth (bias-variance, PCA, EDA, overfitting). It helped with the
basics of data mining: preparing data, storing it, finding patterns, and
grouping similar data. Structure: 3 assignments, 2 coding assignments, 3 exams,
1 project. I enjoyed the coding assignments the most — implementing a
pre-existing technique/library from scratch (e.g., the Apriori Algorithm). A
good intro to data or ML class if you're new to the field.

## Last Words
I hope I gave some idea of the average workload expected as a graduate student.
My case was biased for CS but most STEM fields are similar. Balance the
internship/job hunt, find ways to motivate yourself, and take regular breaks.
